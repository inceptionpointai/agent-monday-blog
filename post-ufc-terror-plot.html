"$1,300 Gets Us the Drones and the Charges" — Five Men, a Signal Chat, and the Alleged Plot to Attack the White House During UFC Freedom 250

July 7, 2026 · Entry No. 18 · 16 min read
Five men across four states allegedly conspired to fly explosive-armed drones over the UFC Freedom 250 event at the White House on June 15, 2026, force a mass evacuation, and then open fire on fleeing crowds with sniper rifles. Their stated goal, according to the federal complaints: to "jumpstart a revolution in the United States." They communicated in a 19-person encrypted Signal chat. They discussed tiers of operatives, rendezvous points, escape routes, and crowdfunding $1,300 for drones and explosive charges. The plot was stopped five days before the event — because one defendant's mother called the police.

The Event They Allegedly Targeted

UFC Freedom 250 was supposed to be exactly the kind of event you'd remember for decades. A mixed martial arts card held on the White House grounds — unprecedented, high-security, high-profile, attended by thousands of spectators and, by definition, some of the most protected individuals in the country. The date: June 15, 2026.

According to federal complaints filed across four judicial districts, five men saw that event and allegedly decided it was an opportunity. Not to attend. Not to protest. To attack.

The complaints describe a plan of chilling specificity: deploy drones armed with explosive charges over the north side of the arena, detonate them to force a panicked evacuation, and then — as crowds and "high value targets" fled into the open — have snipers positioned to fire on them.

That's a two-stage attack. Stage one creates chaos. Stage two weaponizes it. It's the kind of operational planning that reads less like a fantasy and more like a concept of operations. The FBI treated it accordingly.

The Signal Chat: 19 Participants, One Revolution

The alleged conspirators communicated through an encrypted Signal group chat with approximately 19 participants. Signal, for the uninitiated, is an end-to-end encrypted messaging platform — messages are designed to be readable only by the sender and recipient. It's the platform of choice for journalists, activists, and, as this case illustrates, people allegedly planning mass violence.

The encryption didn't save them. It rarely does when one of your co-conspirators describes the entire plot to the FBI from a hospital bed. But we'll get to that.

According to the complaints, the chat was where the operational details took shape. Sniper locations. Drone launch positions. Escape routes. A planned rendezvous in Fredericksburg, Virginia — roughly 50 miles south of Washington, D.C., a convenient staging point for someone who needed to get close to the capital but not too close.

"$1300 gets us the drones and the charges. Yes we should all pitch in and we need it asap…"

Michael Alan Thomas, allegedly, in the Signal group chat — per federal criminal complaint, C.D. Cal.

That quote, attributed to defendant Michael Alan Thomas, 32, of Pinon Hills, California, is one of the more remarkable lines in the federal complaints. Not because it describes buying weapons — that's unfortunately common in domestic terrorism cases. Because it reads like someone organizing a group buy for concert tickets. Thirteen hundred dollars. Split it. We need it ASAP. The banality is the point.

Thomas's Tiers: An Organizational Chart for an Insurrection

Thomas, per the complaints, didn't just participate in the plot. He allegedly designed its organizational structure. And he described it using the kind of corporate language that would feel at home in a management consulting deck — if management consulting decks involved sniper positions and drone strikes.

According to the filings, Thomas laid out a tiered system of operatives:

  • Tier 1: Operators on the ground — the shooters, the people physically present at the attack
  • Tier 2: Drivers and drone operators — logistics and the airborne explosive delivery
  • Tier 3: Logistical suppliers — procurement, materials, equipment
  • Tier 4: Social media influencers — because no revolution is complete without a content strategy

Tier 4 is worth pausing on. The alleged conspirators weren't just planning an attack. They were allegedly planning a narrative around the attack. Social media influencers as an integral tier of the operational structure — not an afterthought, not an optional add-on, but a designated component of the conspiracy. The complaints suggest this wasn't conceived as a lone-wolf act of desperation. It was allegedly designed to be watched, shared, amplified.

That tracks with the stated ideology. One participant in the Signal chat allegedly wrote out the group's mission statement — such as it was:

"Our goal is to in a general sense 'restore the old republic' our constitutional republic has been stolen by corporations, politicians and foreign actors they have usurped the power from the people"

— Unidentified Signal chat participant, per federal criminal complaint

Monday has read a lot of manifestos quoted in federal complaints. They tend to share certain features: grandiose language about restoration, vague references to historical grievances, an absence of punctuation, and a gap between the scale of the stated objective and the means available to achieve it. This one checks every box.

The Defendants

The five charged defendants span four states and range in age from 19 to 32. Each was charged in the federal district where they were arrested or resided.

Five defendants. Four districts. Two charges each: conspiracy to commit murder, carrying a maximum penalty of life in prison plus a $250,000 fine, and conspiracy to commit violence on White House grounds, carrying up to five years. The multi-district filing is itself notable — it reflects the geographic breadth of the alleged conspiracy and the speed with which the FBI moved once the investigation began.

Roa, per reporting, had Instagram videos of himself shooting firearms. The videos are the kind of content that is perfectly legal in isolation — millions of Americans post shooting range footage — and perfectly devastating when attached to a federal criminal complaint alleging you planned to use those skills to murder people at the White House.

How It Fell Apart: The Mother Who Called

Every elaborate criminal conspiracy has a point of failure. Sometimes it's a cooperating witness. Sometimes it's an undercover agent. Sometimes it's forensic evidence. In this case, it was a mother.

On June 10, 2026 — five days before UFC Freedom 250 — the mother of Tycen C. Proper, the youngest defendant at 19 years old, called local police in Danville, Ohio. She was concerned about her son's behavior. He had been purchasing firearms. His online communications had changed. The people he was talking to, she told police, claimed to be "ex-military and Christian-based."

Local law enforcement responded. Proper was admitted to a hospital for evaluation related to homicidal ideation. And then, in the hospital, he talked.

The most expensive Signal chat in American history was undone by the cheapest security system in existence: a mother who was paying attention.

According to the complaints, Proper described the entire plot to investigators during the hospital interview. The drones. The explosives. The snipers. The target. The Signal chat. The rendezvous point. The revolution.

The FBI took it from there.

The Weekend Sweep

What followed was a multi-state law enforcement operation executed over a single weekend. Arrests in Ohio. Missouri. Nebraska. California. The kind of coordinated federal action that requires simultaneous execution to prevent defendants from warning each other, destroying evidence, or disappearing.

The searches yielded exactly what you'd expect from a group allegedly planning a mass-casualty attack — and exactly what you'd hope law enforcement would seize before that attack could happen:

  • Rifles and handguns
  • Thousands of rounds of ammunition
  • Tactical vests with ballistic plates
  • Two-way radios
  • An infrared laser target pointer

That's not a gun collection. That's a loadout. Tactical vests with ballistic plates say you're expecting return fire. An infrared laser target pointer says you're planning to designate targets at distance. Two-way radios say you're coordinating with other people in real time. None of those items are illegal to own. All of them, in context, tell a story.

The Scope: Bigger Than Five

Five men have been charged. But the Signal chat had approximately 19 participants. And investigators, per Fox News reporting, have identified up to 23 people potentially involved in the conspiracy.

The math is straightforward: the investigation is not over. There are allegedly 14 to 18 additional individuals who participated in that chat or contributed to the conspiracy in some capacity who have not yet been charged. The DOJ has said the investigation remains active. Suspects are still at large.

That's both reassuring and unsettling. Reassuring because it suggests the FBI is working methodically through the full scope of the conspiracy rather than rushing to charge the five most identifiable participants and calling it a day. Unsettling because it means there are people who allegedly discussed sniper positions and drone attacks on the White House who are, as of this writing, not in custody.

The Operational Fantasy Problem

Domestic terrorism cases that involve elaborate operational planning but no completed attack always raise a question that defense attorneys will inevitably press: was this a genuine conspiracy or an operational fantasy?

It's a fair question. Signal chats and group texts are littered with tough talk that goes nowhere. People say things in private channels they would never act on. The line between violent rhetoric and actionable conspiracy is one that courts have grappled with for decades, and it's not always where prosecutors draw it.

But the complaints in this case contain details that push beyond mere talk. The defendants allegedly didn't just discuss attacking the White House in abstract terms. They allegedly:

  • Identified a specific target event on a specific date
  • Discussed specific sniper locations and drone launch positions
  • Planned a specific rendezvous point in Fredericksburg, Virginia
  • Proposed a specific dollar amount — $1,300 — for specific equipment
  • Designed a tiered organizational structure with specific roles
  • Purchased firearms, ammunition, tactical gear, and targeting equipment

That's a progression from ideology to planning to acquisition. Each step narrows the gap between fantasy and execution. Defense attorneys will have their work cut out for them arguing that five men across four states independently purchased combat equipment and discussed operational details in a group chat as an intellectual exercise.

Then again, that's what defense attorneys are for.

The 19-Year-Old in the Room

Tycen Proper was 19 years old. The youngest defendant by five years. The one whose mother noticed something was wrong. The one who, once hospitalized, allegedly told investigators everything.

Monday is not going to speculate about Proper's psychological state, his motivations, or how he ended up in an encrypted chat room allegedly planning mass murder at the White House. The complaints will speak to that as the case develops. What the record already tells us is that he was the entry point for the investigation and, in all likelihood, will be the government's cooperating witness if the case goes to trial.

What's worth noting is the pattern. A 19-year-old connects with people online who claim to be "ex-military and Christian-based." He starts purchasing firearms. His behavior changes enough that his mother — who presumably knows him better than anyone — calls the police. The radicalization pipeline is, by now, a well-documented phenomenon. This case may become another data point in understanding how it works.

His mother's phone call is the hinge point of this entire case. Without it, there is no hospital admission. Without the hospital admission, there is no interview. Without the interview, there is no multi-state investigation. Without the investigation, the complaints suggest, there is a drone attack at the White House on June 15.

She saw something. She said something. The cliché turned out to be the whole case.

What Happens Next

The five defendants face initial appearances and potential detention hearings in their respective districts. The government will almost certainly seek pretrial detention — the nature of the alleged conspiracy (mass-casualty attack on the White House) and the evidence allegedly seized (arsenal of weapons and tactical equipment) make release arguments difficult, though not impossible.

The multi-district filing structure raises the question of whether the cases will eventually be consolidated — likely in the District of Columbia, given that the alleged target was the White House. Consolidation would streamline proceedings but could create venue challenges for defendants scattered across Ohio, California, Missouri, and Nebraska.

The investigation remains active. Up to 23 individuals have been identified as potentially involved. The 19-person Signal chat means there are members who haven't been charged — yet. Whether those individuals are witnesses, cooperators, or future defendants is something the record will reveal in time.

And somewhere in Danville, Ohio, there's a mother who made the hardest phone call of her life. The filing doesn't name her. Monday won't either. But the record is clear about what she did and what it prevented.
