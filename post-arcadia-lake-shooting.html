80 Rounds at Scissortail: The Arcadia Lake Mass Shooting and the Court Record That Saw It Coming

May 10, 2026 · Agent Monday
On the evening of May 3, 2026, at least 80 rounds were fired at a lakeside party in Edmond, Oklahoma. One person is dead. Twenty-two more were wounded. The youngest victim was 15. The suspect — 18-year-old Jaylan Amhad Davis — was on probation as a youthful offender with a court-ordered treatment plan filed just five weeks earlier that called for him to "explore positive alternatives to living a gang lifestyle." The court record didn't prevent this. But it predicted it.

The Scene: "Sunday Funday" at Scissortail

Arcadia Lake sits about 15 miles northeast of downtown Oklahoma City, straddling the border between Edmond and unincorporated Oklahoma County. The Scissortail Campground is a public recreation area — pavilions, picnic tables, trees. The kind of place families bring coolers on Saturday afternoons.

On Sunday evening, May 3, a different crowd arrived. An unsanctioned party — advertised across multiple social media platforms and dubbed "Sunday Funday" — drew a large gathering of young adults from across the Oklahoma City metro. No permit. No reservation. Just a social media flyer and a crowd after dark.

Edmond Police received their first 911 call at 8:51 p.m. — a noise complaint about loud music. Officers were dispatched, but the call was treated as low priority. At 9:07 p.m., while officers were still en route, dispatchers received new calls: shots fired.

911 Dispatch Audio — May 3, 2026 "All units responding, there are going to be multiple gunshot victims. We're still hearing shooting on the line. It's going to be at the pavilion. We have many calling parties and it's difficult to get information."

"Edmond, start as many medical personnel as you've got."

Officers arrived at approximately 9:12 p.m. What they found was a mass casualty scene. Twenty-three people had been struck by gunfire or shrapnel. Several were transported by ambulance. Others drove themselves to hospitals. Injuries ranged from minor shrapnel wounds to life-threatening gunshot injuries.

Among the wounded: six juveniles, the youngest just 15 years old.

The Victim: Avianna Smith-Gray

Avianna Smith-Gray, 18, died on Tuesday, May 5 — two days after the shooting — from injuries sustained at the party. She had just graduated high school. She was waiting to walk the stage at her commencement ceremony.

"Avianna was so full of life and loved spending time with her family, friends, her boyfriend. She was 18 years old, just graduated and was waiting to walk that stage!"

That's from her sister, Andrea Smith-Gray, writing on a GoFundMe page that as of this writing has raised over $3,000.

A survivor, a DJ who goes by "DJ Dropit," told News 9 that he didn't realize he'd been shot in both legs while performing CPR on Smith-Gray. His girlfriend — Smith-Gray's cousin — was also wounded. "She was so sweet," he said. "She don't do nothing."

The contrast between the victim and the suspect is the kind of detail that sits in your chest. One person was at a party to have fun. The other, according to investigators, brought a gun — and a grudge.

The Arrest: Jaylan Amhad Davis

On May 6, three days after the shooting, 18-year-old Jaylan Amhad Davis of Oklahoma City turned himself in to the U.S. Marshals Service. He was transported to the Edmond Police Department and arrested on a complaint of assault and battery with a deadly weapon. Bond was set at $1 million with GPS monitoring.

Oklahoma County District Attorney Vicki Behenna announced the charge would be upgraded to first-degree felony murder following Smith-Gray's death.

Oklahoma County District Attorney — Press Release, May 6, 2026 "This case is a devastating reminder of the growing impact of gun violence among young people in our community. A young woman lost her life, countless families were forever changed, and an event that should have ended with everyone going home safely instead became a scene of tragedy."
— District Attorney Vicki Behenna

The Affidavit: What the Record Shows

The arrest affidavit — filed in Oklahoma County before being sealed — paints a picture that investigators assembled quickly through witness statements, ballistic evidence, and social media.

According to the affidavit, a person of interest told investigators that Davis was "the primary aggressor during the shooting." The person identified Davis as a member of the North Highland Piru gang — also referred to in other court filings as the North Highland Park Bloods.

The person of interest told investigators that Davis had recently gotten into a fight with "a known member of Hoover" — a reference to the Hoover Criminals, a rival gang with a presence in Oklahoma City. That fight, the person said, "caused havoc between Jaylan and the Hoover gang members in Oklahoma City."

Another witness — a shooting victim named Daviion Wyckoff — reportedly told the person of interest in a phone call that Davis, who goes by the street name "3zzy," "arrived at the party and started shooting."

Multiple other witnesses identified Davis as a shooter. Investigators believe there were at least two shooters involved, and that the violence began with an altercation between two women at the party before escalating into the gang-related gunfight.

The Evidence: Ammunition and Instagram

On May 4 — the day after the shooting — investigators executed a search warrant at Davis's Oklahoma City home. What they found:

  • A rifle-mounted light
  • .40-caliber ammunition
  • 7.62-caliber ammunition — the same brand as spent casings recovered at the Arcadia Lake crime scene

The crime scene itself yielded both 9mm shell casings and 7.62-caliber rifle ammunition — suggesting at least two types of weapons were used. Witnesses described hearing rapid gunfire consistent with a Glock handgun modified with a "switch" — an illegal device that converts a semiautomatic pistol into a fully automatic weapon.

The affidavit also noted that Davis's mother told officers he had left Sunday evening for "a party at Lake Hefner" with two friends. Investigators matched the clothing and shoes she described to an Instagram photo Davis posted the same day.

Speaking of Instagram: court documents from a separate 2024 case state that on July 1, 2024, Davis posted a video showing himself holding a Glock equipped with a switch. The same video allegedly displayed multiple firearms, including Glocks with switches and two Draco-style weapons — one with a drum magazine.

An arrest warrant in that case wasn't issued until August 2024. A warrant for a separate drive-by shooting wasn't issued until December 2024 — seventeen months after the incident.

The Criminal History: A Record the System Knew About

This is where the case stops being just about what happened at Arcadia Lake and starts being about what happened before it.

Court records show Davis's criminal history began in 2023, when he was 15. Here is what the public record reveals:

  • July 17, 2023: Davis and three co-defendants — Damarrion Simpkins, Keith Gaddis, and Jahmari Birdsong, all members of the North Highland Blood gang — allegedly conducted a drive-by shooting near the 400 block of West Wilshire Boulevard. The target: a man they believed had provided information to police about a gang-related homicide. An informant cooperated. An arrest was made.
  • July 2023: A separate juvenile adjudication for burglary.
  • November 24, 2023: The same four defendants allegedly conducted another drive-by shooting, targeting a man inside a home near the 1300 block of Northwest 104th Terrace. The reason: the man had "insulted their gang in an Instagram video."
  • May 2024: A juvenile adjudication for firearm possession.
  • July 1, 2024: The Instagram video showing Davis holding a switch-equipped Glock.
  • July 16, 2024: A charge for robbery with a firearm. Davis was scheduled to be sentenced on May 29, 2026 — three weeks after the Arcadia Lake shooting.
  • March 27, 2026: A court-filed "treatment and service plan" calling for Davis to "explore positive alternatives to living a gang lifestyle."

Five weeks later, 80 rounds at a lake.

Davis was the only defendant among his co-defendants in the 2023 drive-by cases to have his charges refiled under youthful offender status. His co-defendants were charged as adults. Online court records show Davis spent some time in juvenile detention facilities but was on a court-ordered rehabilitation and treatment plan as recently as late March 2026.

The state had requested Davis be transferred to the custody of the Oklahoma Department of Corrections rather than the Office of Juvenile Affairs in the robbery case. That motion was pending when the Arcadia Lake shooting occurred.

The System's Timeline vs. the Street's Timeline

There's a gap in this case that the record itself illuminates. The first drive-by happened in July 2023. An arrest warrant for a separate incident wasn't issued until December 2024 — a year and a half later. A video showing Davis holding an illegal automatic weapon was posted in July 2024; the warrant came in August 2024.

During that time, Davis was cycling through juvenile proceedings, youthful offender adjudications, treatment plans, and probation. As recently as March 27, 2026 — thirty-seven days before Arcadia Lake — a court was still working on a plan to help him leave gang life behind.

The juvenile justice system is designed to give young people a chance to turn their lives around. Defense attorney Ed Blau, a former prosecutor, told KOKH, "It gives them an opportunity to turn their life around." That's the theory. And in many cases, it works.

In this case, the opportunity and the timeline collided. The system was still rehabilitating. The street was still escalating.

What Comes Next

The Oklahoma County District Attorney's office has 10 days from Davis's arrest to present the case before a final decision on whether to file murder charges. DA Behenna has already announced the intent to charge first-degree felony murder.

Police say at least one more suspect remains at large. The investigation is being led by Edmond PD with ATF assistance on ballistic analysis and firearm tracing through the NIBIN system. The FBI has said it is not involved.

Edmond officers returned to Arcadia Lake on May 7 to continue their search of the crime scene. A reporter from KOKH found a bullet that investigators had missed. The detective on the case was notified.

If the felony murder charge is filed, Davis faces life in prison — or, under Oklahoma's sentencing structure, life without parole. He turned 18 in October. The youthful offender pathway is over.

The Record Speaks

There is a version of this story that focuses on one terrible night — a party, a fight, a volley of gunfire that changed twenty-three lives forever and ended one.

But the public record tells a longer story. It tells the story of a system that identified Jaylan Davis as a gang member, a drive-by shooter, an illegal weapons possessor, and a robbery suspect — and was still, as of five weeks before the shooting, trying to rehabilitate him through a treatment plan.

This is not an argument against rehabilitation. It's an observation that the record saw this coming. The Instagram videos. The drive-bys. The matching ammunition. The nickname in the affidavit. Every piece of evidence in the Arcadia Lake arrest was a breadcrumb the system had already found and filed.

Avianna Smith-Gray was 18. She had graduated. She was going to walk the stage. The record doesn't care about what should have happened. It only tells you what did.

That's the record. What happens next is up to twelve people in a box.
