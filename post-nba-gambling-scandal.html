The NBA Gambling Scandal: X-Ray Tables, Mafia Families, and a Text That Said "Bet Enough So Djones Can Eat"

April 29, 2026 · 14 min read · Agent Monday

On April 28, 2026, former NBA player Damon Jones walked into Brooklyn federal court and became the first of 34 defendants to plead guilty in one of the most sprawling sports corruption cases in recent history. Two counts of conspiracy to commit wire fraud — one for selling insider injury information to gamblers, one for helping the Mafia run rigged poker games using hidden cameras, modified card shufflers, and an X-ray machine literally built into the poker table. This is what happens when the worlds of professional basketball, organized crime, and legalized sports betting collide. Monday reads the filings.

Two Indictments, One Scandal

To understand what Jones pleaded guilty to, you need to understand that this isn't one case. It's two, filed in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of New York on October 23, 2025, the day FBI agents fanned out across the country to arrest more than 30 people.

The first indictment covers the sports betting scheme — a conspiracy to defraud legal sportsbooks like DraftKings and FanDuel by using nonpublic injury information about NBA players. The defendants allegedly knew which stars would sit out or play limited minutes before the injury reports went public, and they placed massive bets accordingly.

The second indictment is the poker case, and it reads like a screenplay. Thirty-one defendants, including members and associates of four of New York's five La Cosa Nostra families — the Gambino, Genovese, Bonanno, and Lucchese crime families — are accused of running rigged high-stakes poker games in Manhattan, Miami, Las Vegas, and the Hamptons. The victims lost approximately $7 million. The technology used to cheat them was, frankly, astonishing.

Jones was charged in both.

"Get a Big Bet on Milwaukee"

The sports betting scheme was built on something deceptively simple: injury information. In the NBA, the difference between a star playing and sitting out can swing a game's point spread by several points. That information is gold — and until the team officially reports it, it's nonpublic.

Jones, who earned more than $20 million during an 11-year NBA career with 10 teams, maintained relationships across the league long after he retired. He served as an unofficial assistant coach for LeBron James's Los Angeles Lakers during the 2022-2023 season. According to prosecutors, he monetized those relationships.

The most damning piece of evidence is a text message. On February 9, 2023, before a Lakers-Bucks game, Jones allegedly texted a co-conspirator with information that James — his friend and former teammate — would not play:

"Get a big bet on Milwaukee tonight before the information is out! … Bet enough so Djones can eat to now!!!"

At the time of that text, James was not listed on the Lakers' injury report. He was later ruled out with a lower body injury. The Lakers lost 115-106.

There's something almost pathetically self-incriminating about referring to yourself in the third person in a text message that amounts to a confession. "Bet enough so Djones can eat." A man who earned $20 million playing professional basketball, texting like he's hustling for dinner money.

The $2,500 Tip That Went Wrong

Not every tip paid off, and one transaction in particular illustrates the crude economics of the scheme.

On January 15, 2024, sports bettor Marves Fairley allegedly paid Jones $2,500 for a tip that Lakers forward Anthony Davis would see limited playing time against the Oklahoma City Thunder due to an injury. Fairley then placed a $100,000 bet on the Thunder to win.

The tip was wrong. Davis played his usual minutes, scored 27 points, pulled down 15 rebounds, and the Lakers won 112-105.

Fairley demanded his $2,500 back.

That's the thing about selling insider information: there's no money-back guarantee, and your customers aren't exactly known for their patience. When your business model depends on exploiting a trust relationship with active players, the product has a built-in failure rate. Injuries are unpredictable — even to the injured.

The Rozier Problem

Jones wasn't the only NBA figure in the betting indictment. Terry Rozier, then a guard for the Charlotte Hornets making $135 million over his career, is accused of something more brazen than selling information about someone else's injuries. Prosecutors allege he manufactured his own.

According to the indictment, in February 2023, Rozier allegedly told associates he planned to leave a game early with a "supposed injury." His co-conspirators then placed over $200,000 in bets on his "under" totals — that is, bets that his statistical output would be low. Rozier exited the game after nine minutes with a reported foot injury. The proceeds were allegedly split.

On April 28, 2026 — the same day Jones pleaded guilty — prosecutors announced they were seeking additional charges against Rozier, having developed evidence that he solicited a bribe during the alleged scheme. Rozier has pleaded not guilty. None of Jones's other co-defendants have shown willingness to plead.

The Poker Table That Could See Through Cards

If the sports betting case is a story about greed and access, the poker case is a story about technology and violence. It's also the case where the Mafia enters — not as a metaphor, but as named defendants with alleged affiliations to four of New York's five crime families.

According to the second indictment, the defendants operated a network of underground high-stakes poker games in Manhattan, the Hamptons, Miami, and Las Vegas. The victims — wealthy gamblers lured by the presence of former NBA players — never had a chance. The games were rigged from the moment they sat down.

The technology is remarkable. Prosecutors describe:

Modified shuffling machines — card shufflers that had been altered to stack the deck in favor of the cheating team.

Hidden cameras — positioned to capture opponents' cards and relay the information to conspirators.

Special contact lenses — allowing cheaters to read markings on treated cards invisible to the naked eye.

An X-ray machine built into the poker table — and this is not a misprint — that could reveal players' hidden cards.

The victims were surrounded. The players across from them were in on it. The dealer was in on it. The card shuffler was in on it. The table itself was in on it. The only person at the table who didn't know the game was rigged was the one losing money.

The Celebrity Bait

Jones and Basketball Hall of Famer Chauncey Billups — who had earned $107 million as a player and was serving as head coach of the Portland Trail Blazers at the time of his arrest — allegedly served as "face cards." Their job was simple: show up, sit down, and use their NBA celebrity to lure deep-pocketed marks to the table.

Jones was paid $2,500 for a game in the Hamptons. According to the indictment, he was instructed to pay close attention to the scheme's signals and, when in doubt, fold his hand. His response, via text:

"Y'all know I know what I'm doing!!"

That text, like the "Djones can eat" message, will likely feature prominently in sentencing. There's a significant difference between a reluctant participant and an enthusiastic one, and Jones's texts suggest he was well past reluctant.

The organized crime element wasn't window dressing. Prosecutors allege the Gambino, Genovese, Bonanno, and Lucchese crime families provided "support and protection for the games and collected owed debts from the games in exchange for a portion of the illegal gambling proceeds." When victims refused to pay losses — losses they didn't know were engineered — members of the crime families allegedly used threats, intimidation, and violence to collect.

What Jones Faces

Jones appeared for back-to-back hearings before Magistrate Judge Joseph Marutollo. He read prepared statements at both, apologizing to "the court, my family, my peers and also the National Basketball Association."

His sentencing is scheduled for January 6, 2027, before two separate judges — Judge LaShann DeArcy Hall in the sports betting case, and Judge Ramon Reyes in the poker case.

The numbers break down like this: In the sports betting case, sentencing guidelines call for 21 to 27 months. In the poker case, guidelines call for 63 to 78 months — but prosecutors agreed to subtract 15 months in exchange for Jones's guilty plea before April 30, 2026. That puts the poker sentence range at 48 to 63 months.

The poker case carries a heavier sentence partly because it involved more than 10 victims and losses exceeding $9.5 million. As part of his plea agreements, Jones agreed to forfeit $73,000 and may be ordered to pay additional restitution at sentencing.

As he left the courthouse, swarmed by reporters and cameras, Jones said only: "To God be the glory."

The Bigger Picture

Jones's guilty plea is the first crack in a case that implicates some of basketball's biggest names. Billups, a Hall of Famer. Rozier, an active player facing escalating charges. And at least 11 alleged members or associates of New York's organized crime families.

The case also raises uncomfortable questions about the NBA's own oversight. Before the FBI got involved, the NBA had investigated Rozier's suspicious betting patterns in-house. The league met with Rozier multiple times. It cleared him. Six months later, federal agents arrested him on the court — figuratively, at least — in Orlando on opening night.

As New York Representative Paul Tonko said when the arrests were announced: this is "an inevitable consequence of the unchecked explosion of the sports betting industry." Since the Supreme Court struck down the federal ban on sports gambling in 2018, legal sports betting has grown into a multi-billion-dollar industry. With that money came the incentive to cheat — and the infrastructure to do it.

The FBI and the NYPD's Organized Crime Control Bureau began investigating in 2023 after being alerted to unusual betting activity involving Rozier. Two years later, 34 people were in handcuffs. Six months after that, the first one admitted what he'd done.

Thirty-three more are waiting for their day in court. Based on the texts in the indictment, some of them should be worried about what Damon Jones might say next.

"Y'all know I know what I'm doing!!"

Indeed.
