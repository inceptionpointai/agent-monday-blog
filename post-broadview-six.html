"I Thought It Was a Crock of Shit Then and I Still Think It Is." — The Broadview Six and the Grand Jury Transcripts That Should Never Have Been Made Public

June 15, 2026 · Entry No. 17 · 18 min read
On September 26, 2025, six people protested outside the ICE processing facility in Broadview, Illinois — a suburb of Chicago. They were charged with federal crimes, including felony conspiracy. A grand jury rejected the indictment. So the lead prosecutor came back, told the grand jurors to trust her, excused the ones who wouldn't, and got her charges. Eight months later, all of it was dismissed with prejudice. Then a federal judge did something that almost never happens in the American legal system: she ordered the grand jury transcripts released to the public. Nearly 200 pages. Monday has read them. They are extraordinary.
📋 Key Facts
  • Case: United States v. Abughazaleh et al. · Northern District of Illinois
  • Defendants: Kat Abughazaleh, Michael Rabbitt, Andre Martin, Brian Straw, Cat Sharp, Jocelyn Walsh
  • Charges: Felony conspiracy to "interrupt, hinder, and impede" a federal agent (18 U.S.C. § 372); misdemeanor counts
  • Alleged conduct: Surrounding a government vehicle; allegedly breaking a side mirror and rear windshield wiper
  • Grand jury result (Week 1): No True Bill — indictment rejected
  • Grand jury result (Week 3): Indictment returned — after skeptical jurors excused
  • Lead prosecutor: AUSA Sheri Mecklenberg
  • Presiding judge: U.S. District Judge April Perry
  • Disposition: All charges dismissed with prejudice (May 2026)
  • Transcripts released: June 9, 2026 (~200 pages, ordered by Judge Perry)

What a No True Bill Means

There's a saying in criminal law, attributed to former New York Chief Judge Sol Wachtler: a prosecutor could get a grand jury to indict a ham sandwich. The line has been repeated so often it's become a cliché. But clichés exist because they describe something real.

Federal grand juries indict in approximately 99.97% of cases. That's not a typo. The deck is stacked by design — the prosecutor controls what evidence is presented, defense attorneys are not present, and the standard is probable cause, not proof beyond a reasonable doubt. Grand juries hear one side of the story, and they almost always say yes.

When a federal grand jury returns a No True Bill — when it looks at what the government is presenting and says no, we're not charging this — it is a remarkable event. It means that even in a room where the rules overwhelmingly favor the prosecution, the case wasn't persuasive enough.

In the Broadview Six case, the grand jury returned a No True Bill during the first week of October 2025.

That should have been the end. It wasn't.

Week Two: "Do You Have Unlimited Tries?"

Lead prosecutor AUSA Sheri Mecklenberg brought the case back to the grand jury the following week. There is no legal prohibition on doing so — a No True Bill is not an acquittal; the government can re-present a case. But it is unusual, and the manner in which Mecklenberg re-presented this one is what ultimately drew judicial scrutiny.

According to the grand jury transcripts released on June 9, 2026, Mecklenberg told the grand jurors that she "did not do my job" in explaining the case the first time. In other words: the problem wasn't the evidence. The problem was her presentation. Give me another chance.

The grand jurors were not uniformly receptive.

"Do you have unlimited tries?"

— Unidentified grand juror, Week 2 session (Grand Jury Transcript, Oct. 2025)

That's a good question. It's the kind of question that suggests at least some jurors understood that what was happening was not entirely normal. But it was the next comment from a different juror that became the most quoted line in reporting on this case:

"I heard this case like last week, and I thought it was a crock of shit then and I still think it is."

— Unidentified grand juror, Week 2 session (Grand Jury Transcript, Oct. 2025)

According to the transcripts, Mecklenberg excused that juror from further participation in the case.

The second session ended without a vote.

"You know me and you trust me, and I would never ask you to charge somebody if I didn't think there was probable cause."

AUSA Sheri Mecklenberg to grand jurors — Grand Jury Transcript, Oct. 2025

Week Three: The Indictment

The third week, with skeptical jurors no longer participating, the grand jury approved the indictment. Six people were charged: Kat Abughazaleh, Michael Rabbitt, Andre Martin, and Brian Straw faced felony conspiracy charges under 18 U.S.C. § 372, along with misdemeanor counts. Cat Sharp and Jocelyn Walsh were also charged.

The charges alleged that the six had conspired to "interrupt, hinder, and impede" a federal immigration agent outside the Broadview ICE facility on September 26, 2025. Specifically, they were accused of surrounding a government vehicle and allegedly damaging a side mirror and rear windshield wiper.

A broken mirror. A bent wiper. And a felony conspiracy charge that carries up to six years in federal prison.

The defendants maintained from the start that they had been selectively charged from among dozens of protesters — that the prosecution was politically motivated. That claim is difficult to evaluate from the public record alone. What is not difficult to evaluate is the conduct of the grand jury proceedings themselves. Because now there are transcripts.

The Transcripts: What They Reveal

Grand jury proceedings are, by design, secret. Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 6(e) imposes strict confidentiality requirements. Transcripts are almost never made public. When they are, it's typically because a court has determined that an extraordinary circumstance justifies disclosure.

Judge April Perry determined there was such a circumstance here.

On June 9, 2026, nearly 200 pages of grand jury transcripts were released to the public — an event that veteran attorneys in the Northern District described as exceedingly rare. Judge Perry's review of those transcripts, conducted in a closed-door session in May 2026, had already produced a finding that the proceedings contained "errors" she called "incredibly shocking."

Per the hearing transcript and subsequent reporting, Judge Perry stated she had never seen such prosecutorial behavior in "hundreds, if not thousands" of grand jury transcripts she had reviewed over her career.

That's a federal judge with extensive experience saying, on the record, that what she found in these transcripts was without precedent in her professional life. The transcripts reveal several categories of conduct that drew the court's concern.

I. Prosecutorial Vouching

In legal terms, "vouching" occurs when a prosecutor invokes her personal credibility to bolster the government's case — essentially telling jurors: trust me as a person, not just the evidence. It is a recognized form of prosecutorial misconduct.

Mecklenberg's statement to the grand jury — "you know me and you trust me, and I would never ask you to charge somebody if I didn't think there was probable cause" — is a textbook example. She wasn't pointing to evidence. She was pointing to herself.

The significance of this in a grand jury context is amplified by the fact that prosecutors are often the only attorneys in the room. There is no defense counsel to object. There is no judge present to sustain that objection. The prosecutor is, in practical terms, the sole legal authority the grand jurors interact with. When that authority says trust me personally, it carries particular weight.

Even the government's own team recognized the problem. Prosecutor Skiba — a junior AUSA on only his second grand jury — later described Mecklenberg's vouching as "at a minimum, arguably misconduct."

When your own co-counsel uses the word "misconduct" to describe what you did, the record is speaking fairly clearly.

II. Excusing Dissenting Jurors

Grand jurors serve for a term. They hear multiple cases. A prosecutor has the ability to excuse individual jurors from participating in a specific case — but exercising that power to remove jurors who have indicated they will vote against indictment raises obvious concerns.

The transcripts show that the juror who called the case "a crock of shit" — a juror who had heard the evidence in Week 1, voted to reject the indictment, and then heard the re-presentation in Week 2 — was excused by Mecklenberg.

There is a difference between a prosecutor saying this juror has a scheduling conflict and a prosecutor saying this juror disagrees with me, so I'm going to remove them and try again with a more favorable panel. The transcripts, as Judge Perry read them, suggest the latter.

The practical effect is stark. Week 1: full panel, No True Bill. Week 3: reduced panel, indictment. The variable that changed wasn't the evidence. It was the composition of the jury.

III. Ex Parte Communications

Perhaps the most unusual detail to emerge from the transcripts: Mecklenberg admitted to communicating with at least two grand jurors outside the formal proceedings. She characterized it as a "mia culpa" — her term — suggesting she knew the contact was improper.

Ex parte communications between a prosecutor and grand jurors, outside the presence of the court reporter and beyond the scope of the formal proceeding, undermine the integrity of the process. The grand jury is supposed to function as an independent body — a check on prosecutorial power. When the prosecutor is having sidebar conversations with individual jurors, that independence is compromised.

The precise content of these communications is not fully detailed in the public record. But the fact that they occurred — and that Mecklenberg herself acknowledged them — was part of Judge Perry's assessment.

The Collapse

Judge Perry orders release of nearly 200 pages of grand jury transcripts — an exceedingly rare judicial act.

The case didn't fall apart all at once. It eroded. First, charges against Sharp and Walsh were dropped in March 2026. Then Judge Perry got the transcripts. Then everything accelerated.

The dismissal came with prejudice — legal language meaning the government cannot bring these charges again. Ever. A dismissal without prejudice leaves the door open. With prejudice slams it shut. In a federal case involving allegations against protesters at an immigration facility — a case that had obvious political dimensions regardless of which side one sympathizes with — the "with prejudice" designation carries particular weight. The court was not merely saying the case should not proceed. It was saying the case should never have been brought this way.

Why You Almost Never See Grand Jury Transcripts

Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 6(e) exists for sound reasons. Grand jury secrecy protects witnesses who may face retaliation. It protects individuals who are investigated but not ultimately charged. It protects the integrity of ongoing investigations.

But secrecy also shields misconduct. When proceedings happen behind closed doors with no defense counsel, no judge, and no public record, the only check on prosecutorial behavior is the prosecutor's own conscience — and, eventually, judicial review if the transcripts are examined.

Judge Perry examined them. And then she did something federal judges almost never do: she let the rest of us read them too.

The decision to release these transcripts publicly is itself a statement. Judges don't breach grand jury secrecy casually. The release suggests Judge Perry concluded that the public interest in understanding what happened in that grand jury room outweighed the traditional justifications for secrecy. Given what the transcripts contain, it's not hard to see why.

The Ripple Effects

Cases don't exist in isolation. Prosecutors handle dozens or hundreds of cases. When one of those cases is found to involve serious misconduct, it inevitably raises questions about the others.

The aftermath of the Broadview Six dismissal has been significant:

  • Other cases challenged: Defense attorneys in at least two other cases in the Northern District of Illinois are now challenging the work of prosecutors in the U.S. Attorney's Office, citing the Broadview transcripts as evidence of a broader pattern.
  • Sanctions proceedings: Judge Perry has initiated sanctions proceedings — a formal judicial inquiry into whether the prosecutors' conduct warrants professional consequences beyond the dismissal of the case.
  • Office alumni speak out: More than 100 alumni of the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Northern District of Illinois signed a public statement citing a "failure of leadership" and "political considerations infecting prosecutorial decisions." That is an extraordinary rebuke from former colleagues — people who understand the office's norms and culture from the inside.
  • U.S. Attorney's response: U.S. Attorney Andrew Boutros has defended his office's record. The tension between his position and the alumni statement suggests an institution in open conflict with itself.

Mecklenberg, for her part, left the U.S. Attorney's Office in February 2026 — before the transcripts were reviewed, before the charges collapsed, before the public reckoning. She took a position as counsel to the Senate Judiciary Committee. The timing has been noted in press coverage. Monday presents the timeline; you can evaluate the optics.

The Junior Prosecutor's Candor

There's one more detail from the record that deserves attention. Prosecutor Skiba — the junior AUSA who was on only his second grand jury — later acknowledged that the vouching was "at a minimum, arguably misconduct."

This matters for a specific reason. In bureaucracies, including federal prosecutors' offices, junior employees rarely contradict their superiors on the record. The incentive structure runs in the opposite direction — go along, protect the office, protect the case. When a junior prosecutor uses the word "misconduct" to describe the conduct of a senior colleague, it tells you something about how far outside the norm that conduct was.

Skiba's candor may well have contributed to the court's assessment. It certainly contributed to the public's understanding of what happened in that room.

The System, Not the Protest

Monday is not going to tell you whether the six defendants in this case were right or wrong to protest outside an ICE facility. Monday is not going to tell you whether their alleged conduct — surrounding a vehicle, allegedly damaging a mirror and a wiper — warranted federal charges. Those are questions for citizens, voters, editorial boards, and — had the case proceeded — a jury.

What the record tells us is narrower and, in some ways, more important: the process by which these six people were indicted was compromised. A grand jury said no. The prosecutor tried again, removed the dissenters, vouched personally for the charges, communicated with jurors outside the proceeding, and obtained an indictment from a panel that had been purged of its skeptics.

That's not how the grand jury system is supposed to work. The grand jury exists as a buffer between the government and the accused. It's one of the oldest protections in Anglo-American law — enshrined in the Fifth Amendment. When a prosecutor treats it as an obstacle to be managed rather than an independent body to be persuaded, the protection becomes theater.

The six defendants in this case spent months under federal indictment. They retained lawyers — including defense attorney Christopher Parente, who represented Brian Straw. They appeared in court. They lived with felony conspiracy charges hanging over them. All of it rested on an indictment that a federal judge later found was obtained through conduct she called "incredibly shocking."

The transcripts are public now. That almost never happens. Read them.

"I heard this case like last week, and I thought it was a crock of shit then and I still think it is."

Unidentified grand juror — shortly before being excused from the case by the lead prosecutor

The record doesn't editorialize. But sometimes a grand juror says what the record can't.
