"There Are No Words I Can Say" — Rex Heuermann Gets Life Without Parole, and the Gilgo Beach Case Becomes a Closed Record

On June 17, 2026, Rex Heuermann — the 62-year-old Massapequa Park architect who admitted to strangling eight women over a span of at least seventeen years on Long Island — stood in a Suffolk County courtroom and received three consecutive life sentences without the possibility of parole, plus four additional consecutive terms of 25 years to life. Thirteen family members delivered victim impact statements. One told him she hoped he'd suffer. Another, who was three years old when her mother disappeared, said her world was shattered. The judge asked if Heuermann was sorry. "Yes I am," he said. The judge called him "disgusting" and "despicable." Then the courtroom applauded. Monday has covered this case from the beginning — the endgame, the plea, and now the sentence. The record is closed.
📋 Case Summary
  • Defendant: Rex A. Heuermann, 62, of Massapequa Park, New York — Manhattan architect
  • Court: Suffolk County Supreme Court, Riverhead, New York
  • Judge: Hon. Timothy Mazzei
  • Plea: Guilty (entered April 2026) — 3 counts first-degree murder, 4 counts second-degree murder; admitted to uncharged killing of Karen Vergata
  • Sentence: Three consecutive life terms without parole (Barthelemy, Waterman, Costello) + four consecutive terms of 25 years to life (Brainard-Barnes, Taylor, Costilla, Mack) = life without parole plus 100 years
  • Victims (8): Melissa Barthelemy, Megan Waterman, Amber Lynn Costello, Maureen Brainard-Barnes, Jessica Taylor, Valerie Mack, Sandra Costilla, Karen Vergata (uncharged, admitted)
  • Time span: 1993–2010 (earliest known victim to last known victim)
  • Plea condition: Cooperation with FBI Behavioral Analysis Unit to assist in identifying other serial killers

The Courtroom

It was packed. Victims' families, law enforcement officers, reporters, and members of a sex workers' rights group who had waited years for this day. In the front, thirteen family members sat with statements they'd written, revised, and agonized over — the kind of documents no one should ever have to draft.

Rex Heuermann sat at the defense table in a rumpled grey suit, blue shirt, and gold tie. He looked straight ahead. He tapped his fingers lightly on the table. He did not make eye contact with the families speaking to him — until one of them demanded it.

"You Can Look at Me While I'm Talking"

Amanda Funderburg was fifteen years old when her sister, Melissa Barthelemy, disappeared in 2009. Days after the disappearance, Funderburg received phone calls from a man — later identified as Heuermann — taunting her about her sister's decomposing body.

On Wednesday, Funderburg stood in the courtroom, turned to face Heuermann, and commanded him to look at her.

"You can look at me while I'm talking — it has been about 17 years since we've spoken."

— Amanda Funderburg, sister of victim Melissa Barthelemy

He glanced in her direction. His eyes were slightly downcast.

"I hope you suffer," she said.

She called him an "ogre" — the same word a witness used to describe the man she saw picking up Amber Costello in 2010, the description that would eventually help investigators identify Heuermann two years ago. A word that followed him from the street to the courtroom.

Sixteen Mother's Days

Liliana Waterman was three years old when her mother, Megan Waterman, vanished in June 2010. A single mom from Maine who had traveled to Long Island. She never came home.

Liliana found out how her mother died by scrolling on her phone. She'd spent her entire life waiting for this day.

"In an instant, my world was shattered. I have spent sixteen Mother's Days without her."

— Liliana Waterman, daughter of victim Megan Waterman

Outside the courthouse after the sentencing, Liliana said simply: "She can finally rest in peace. He can't hurt anybody else."

"A Million Years Isn't Enough"

The impact statements came one after another. Thirteen family members. Each one carrying decades of grief compressed into minutes.

Violet Swager and Jasmine Robinson, cousins of Jessica Taylor — who would have turned 43 on the day of the sentencing — spoke together. Robinson remembered Taylor as "fierce, kind, compassionate, beautiful, and intelligent." She told Heuermann: "You chose small women because you're nothing more than a weak, disgusting coward."

Swager said what the numbers couldn't:

"I can't even put into words the eviscerating hatred I have for you. A million years isn't enough. Nothing will ever make this right."

— Violet Swager, cousin of victim Jessica Taylor

JoAnn Mack, mother of Valerie Mack, whose partial remains were first found in Manorville in 2000 and then at Gilgo Beach more than a decade later, told Heuermann her daughter "had dreams, and you took them all away from her."

Ed Mack, Valerie's adoptive father, addressed the defendant directly: "Mr. Heuermann, you have done horrendous things to Valerie's earthly body, but you have not touched the real Valerie."

Amber Costello's sister, Kimberly Overstreet, called Heuermann "a raging, murdering sex addict."

Megan Waterman's aunt, Elizabeth Meserve, called him "a cowardly opportunist" who "simply wanted to torture and kill."

Maureen Brainard-Barnes' two children — who were seven and one when she disappeared in 2007 — described how her absence shaped their entire lives. Their mother never got to know the adults they became.

Brainard-Barnes' sister, Melissa Cann, sobbed deeply as she described wondering for decades whether she could have done more. Then she caught herself: "That guilt is not mine to carry. It is for Rex and Rex alone."

The Defendant Speaks

When Heuermann indicated he wanted to make a statement, Judge Mazzei's response was immediate: "Stand up!"

Heuermann stood. What followed was twenty words:

"I am responsible for all that was said in this room. There are no words I can say. The words I would say would have no meaning. And I'm going to leave it there."

— Rex Heuermann, at sentencing

The judge was not satisfied.

"Are you at least a little bit sorry for that? Are you at least a little bit sorry for these poor innocent women?"

— Judge Timothy Mazzei

Heuermann nodded. "Yes I am."

The judge's voice rose.

"You are disgusting — a despicable man, if you are a man at all. And you are a coward."

— Judge Timothy Mazzei, before imposing sentence

Then: "Get him out of here."

As Heuermann was led away in handcuffs, the courtroom erupted in chants of "ogre" and applause.

The Sentence

Judge Mazzei imposed the maximum sentence available under New York law:

Sentence Breakdown Three consecutive life sentences without parole:
— Murder of Melissa Barthelemy (first-degree)
— Murder of Megan Waterman (first-degree)
— Murder of Amber Lynn Costello (first-degree)

Four consecutive sentences of 25 years to life:
— Murder of Maureen Brainard-Barnes (second-degree)
— Murder of Jessica Taylor (second-degree)
— Murder of Sandra Costilla (second-degree)
— Murder of Valerie Mack (second-degree)

Admitted killing of Karen Vergata — uncharged per plea agreement

Total: Life without parole + 100 years to life, consecutive

Suffolk County DA Raymond Tierney told reporters afterward that his "greatest wish" was for the victims' families to "enjoy great lives."

"They stood by their loved ones and did a wonderful job," Tierney said. "Now it's time for them to heal."

The Checklist

In 2024, after Heuermann's arrest, prosecutors recovered what they described as a "blueprint" for the killings from his personal computer files. Among the documents: a series of checklists with reminders.

Limit noise. Clean the bodies. Destroy evidence.

This is the detail that distinguishes Heuermann in the record — not just the number of victims, not just the time span, but the methodical, documented nature of the planning. He didn't just kill. He kept instructions for himself about how to do it better.

These files were found on the computer of a man who was, by all outward appearances, a suburban father and professional architect. He designed buildings during the day. According to the record, he planned murders at night. He kept the checklists in the same system where he presumably stored floor plans and client invoices.

The mundanity of the evil is the point. It always is, in these cases.

The Cooperation Clause

One element of the plea deal deserves particular attention. As part of his agreement, Heuermann waived his right to further prosecution in connection with the eight victims — but agreed to cooperate with the FBI's Behavioral Analysis Unit going forward.

The BAU — the same unit known for its work profiling serial killers dating back to the 1970s, the unit that became the basis for Mindhunter and Silence of the Lambs — now has a cooperating serial killer on its roster.

This isn't unprecedented. The BAU has historically interviewed incarcerated serial killers to build behavioral profiles. But having a cooperating subject as part of a plea agreement — someone with a legal obligation to participate — is a different kind of arrangement. It means the FBI can ask questions, request clarifications, and probe the decision-making process of a man who killed methodically over nearly two decades.

Whether that cooperation yields anything actionable is another question. Serial killers cooperate on their own terms, even when they're legally required to. But the clause is in the agreement. It's in the record.

The Ex-Wife's Absence

Asa Ellerup, Heuermann's ex-wife, did not attend the sentencing. Her attorney, Robert Macedonio, said she stayed away "out of respect for those who endured unimaginable loss and suffering" and did not wish her presence "to distract from the purpose of these proceedings."

Ellerup recently revealed that she sleeps in the basement of the Massapequa Park home — the same basement where, according to investigators, the torture and killing took place. She has filed for divorce.

The couple's two adult children also did not attend.

There is no public record of Ellerup or the children being implicated in the crimes. They are, as far as the filings show, among the case's secondary victims — people whose lives were detonated by proximity to a man they thought they knew.

The Three-Year Wait

Since his arrest in July 2023, Heuermann has spent three years in a segregated cell at the Suffolk County Jail in Riverhead. According to Sheriff Errol Toulon, he spent his time alone, reading crime novels, receiving occasional visits from lawyers and family, and — in a detail that feels almost too on the nose — striking up a brief correspondence with the "Happy Face Killer," Keith Jesperson.

A serial killer, in isolation, reading crime fiction and writing to another serial killer. The record doesn't editorialize. Neither does Monday. But the facts speak clearly enough.

Heuermann will now be transferred to a state prison to be determined by the New York Department of Corrections.

What the Record Says

Here's what the record ultimately shows:

  • Eight women — all young sex workers — were strangled, some dismembered, and dumped along Long Island's south shore over at least seventeen years.
  • The case sat cold for over a decade, until a witness description of an "ogre-like" man in a green pickup truck was finally matched in 2022.
  • DNA from a discarded pizza crust in a Manhattan trash can was matched to hair fragments found on the victims' remains.
  • Cellphone location data placed Heuermann near the victims at the times of their disappearances.
  • A digital "blueprint" on his computer contained checklists for the killings — limit noise, clean bodies, destroy evidence.
  • He taunted at least one victim's family by phone after the killing.
  • He lived as a suburban architect and father while methodically targeting vulnerable women.
  • He pleaded guilty to seven murders, admitted to an eighth, and agreed to cooperate with the FBI.
  • He was sentenced to consecutive life terms without parole.

DA Tierney told reporters his greatest wish was for the families to "enjoy great lives." Judge Mazzei told Heuermann he was "disgusting." The families said what needed to be said. The spectators applauded.

And that is the record.

"Justice has been done, but it can't replace what has been taken."

— JoAnn Mack, mother of Valerie Mack

Eight women are dead. Their families have waited, in some cases, more than thirty years. One man will spend the rest of his life in prison. The courtroom applauded, and then it emptied, and the case file was marked closed.

The public record doesn't offer closure. It doesn't heal anything. It just tells you what happened, who was responsible, and what the system did about it.

In this case, it did what it was supposed to do. It just took a very long time.
