"We Will Run This City. I Choose Violence." — The Trinitarios RICO Takedown

June 11, 2026 · Entry No. 15 · 15 min read
Over two years, Operation Paper Machetes has charged 56 alleged members of the Trinitarios — a Dominican-origin transnational gang — in connection with 15 murders, 30 attempted murders, and a drug trafficking empire spanning Massachusetts, Maine, and New Hampshire. The latest superseding indictment, unsealed June 9, 2026, names 26 defendants from the Lawrence, Haverhill, and Boston chapters. The supporting affidavit runs 465 pages. The alleged leader of the Lawrence chapter was recorded saying, "We will run this city. I choose violence." He said it on a phone that was being monitored by the FBI.

This isn't one indictment. It's the culmination of 14 federal cases, across two operational phases, spanning two years — and it resulted in the charging of 56 people. To put that in perspective: by the time the U.S. Attorney for Massachusetts finished her press conference on June 9, the Trinitarios' leadership structure in the state had, in her words, been "fully decimated."

The phrase is not hyperbole. It may actually be an understatement.

What Are the Trinitarios?

The Trinitarios are a Dominican-American gang that originated in the New York City prison system in the 1980s, named after the 19th-century Dominican independence movement La Trinitaria. They have since expanded into a transnational criminal organization with chapters in multiple states and connections back to the Dominican Republic.

In Massachusetts, according to court documents, the gang established chapters in Lawrence, Lynn, Boston, and Haverhill. Each chapter had what the filings describe as a "Cabinet" — a structured leadership group responsible for recruiting new members, enforcing discipline, collecting money for a communal treasury, and supporting incarcerated members and their families.

The gang's organizational formality is striking. According to the indictment, the Trinitarios in Massachusetts operated under a written "Magna Carta" — an actual document that defines the structure, rules, slogans, symbols, colors, and practices of the organization. They held formal induction ceremonies. They administered oaths. They awarded ceremonial beaded necklaces to new members.

They also required prospective members to complete a "mission" before being "blessed" into the organization — a mission that, per the filings, generally meant committing a significant act of violence. A shooting. A beating. A fight with a rival gang member of comparable age and size.

Younger recruits — often high school students from Lawrence and Lynn, many of them recent immigrants from the Dominican Republic — were allegedly tasked with lesser roles during violent operations: standing lookout during shootings, concealing weapons on behalf of full members, transporting firearms after an attack.

Operation Paper Machetes: Two Phases, One Objective

The federal investigation that ultimately produced this week's indictment began in 2024, after a spate of violence in Lynn — four murders and a series of attempted murders and shootings — all allegedly committed by Trinitarios members.

The investigation had two phases. The names alone tell you something about how the government viewed this case.

📄 Operational Phases Phase 1: "Lime-Light" (culminated February 2025)
— 22 leaders and members of the Lynn Chapter charged with RICO conspiracy
— Included the state leader ("Supreme"), Enmanuel Paula-Cabral (a/k/a "Nelfew"), and his second-in-command
— Connected to 6 murders and 11 attempted murders

Phase 2: "Never-Green" (culminated June 2026)
— 29 additional leaders, members, and associates of the Lawrence, Haverhill, and Boston chapters charged
— Connected to 5 additional murders and 19 attempted murders
— 26 defendants in the superseding indictment unsealed June 9

Between the two phases, five members have pleaded guilty. Two have been sentenced — one to 10 years, one to 14. The leader of the Lynn chapter got the 14-year sentence in July 2025.

But even after 22 arrests in Phase 1, the gang didn't slow down. It restructured. According to the filings, Yordali Yorro — the alleged leader of the Lawrence chapter — assumed control of the Haverhill chapter as well. In a conversation recorded by a cooperating witness shortly after the February 2025 arrests, Yorro allegedly discussed the situation with remarkable candor:

"They looking for a face for Lawrence right now. They are not going to find me."

They found him. On the morning of June 9, 2026, federal agents arrested Yorro at his Lawrence home. Inside, investigators allegedly seized high-capacity firearms, large quantities of ammunition, and nearly two kilograms of cocaine and fentanyl.

The record doesn't editorialize. But sometimes the facts speak loudly enough.

The Kidnapping in the Lowell Basement

Among the previously charged cases folded into this investigation is one that reads like a film treatment — except every detail comes from a federal affidavit filed by a Homeland Security special agent.

On the night of May 5, 2024, Isiah Medina, 25, and Rodderith Peralta, 26 — alleged Trinitarios members known as "Ice" and "Mago" — allegedly confronted a drug supplier named Nilbel Ventura Amaro outside his Andover apartment. They were wearing jackets bearing the insignia of the DEA and the FBI.

According to the affidavit, the two pepper-sprayed Ventura Amaro, forced him into a Jeep, and drove him to a basement in Lowell. There, he was allegedly blindfolded with a winter hat, zip-tied, threatened with a syringe, and fed Percocet to keep him sedated.

Three days later, he was released in the Centralville section of Lowell — confused, shoeless, and still partially bound. Witnesses had called 911 the night of the abduction. Medina and Peralta were charged with conspiracy to commit kidnapping in June 2025.

Impersonating federal agents. A three-day hostage situation. A drug supplier as the victim. And two defendants whose alleged street names are "Ice" and "Mago." Again — every word of this is from the filing.

"I Choose Violence" — On a Monitored Line

The 465-page affidavit supporting this investigation — which, to be clear, is longer than most novels — reportedly includes extensive recorded phone calls and conversations captured by cooperating witnesses.

U.S. Attorney Leah Foley highlighted several at her press conference:

  • The alleged Lawrence chapter leader, Yordali Yorro, declared: "We will run this city. I choose violence."
  • One defendant discussed murdering a government cooperator and claimed he would "execute people" if gang members cooperated with federal investigators.
  • Defendants discussed organizational structure openly — identifying members for "security," a "spokesman," an "enforcer," and a "treasurer."
  • Gang members discussed finding locations to administer beatings and shootings. One such beating was captured on video by a cooperating witness.

As Foley put it: "They spoke openly and moved freely without fear of consequences. Until today."

There's a pattern in RICO cases that Monday sees over and over: the organizations that build the most elaborate internal structures — the constitutions, the ceremonies, the hierarchies — are often the ones that generate the most evidence against themselves. When you have a written "Magna Carta," formalized ranks, and members who film music videos holding weapons and flashing gang signs at gas stations, you are essentially building the prosecution's organizational chart for them.

The Music Videos

According to the DOJ press release, Trinitarios members produced music and music videos featuring gang pageantry: members in Trinitarios colors, holding weapons and cash, with lyrics "that boasted about the Trinitarios proclivity for violence and the scope of their successful drug distribution operations." The DOJ released a still image from one such video showing members at a Lawrence gas station, flashing gang signs and holding green bandanas — the gang's colors.

These videos served a dual purpose, per the filings. They were recruitment tools — designed to project power and prosperity to potential members, especially the young Dominican immigrants the gang allegedly targeted. And they were warnings to rival gangs.

They also turned out to be evidence. Quite a lot of evidence, apparently, across 465 pages' worth.

The Drug Pipeline: Lawrence to Maine

The indictment doesn't just allege violence. It describes an alleged drug trafficking enterprise spanning three states. Court documents reference the trafficking of "dozens of kilograms" of fentanyl, cocaine, and methamphetamine from Massachusetts into New Hampshire and Maine.

During the June 9 arrest of Yorro, agents also executed a search warrant at a Trinitarios "stash house" in Tewksbury, Massachusetts. According to the DOJ, they found over 600 grams of fentanyl, 200 grams of cocaine, cutting agents, drug manufacturing and packaging equipment, and three firearms.

The operation also resulted in a companion case: the ATF arrested four individuals in New Hampshire in connection with the firearms trafficking arm of the enterprise.

The Scale

Stand back from the details for a moment and consider the cumulative numbers from this two-year investigation:

  • 56 defendants charged across 14 federal cases
  • 15 murders spanning 2017–2025
  • 30 attempted murders
  • 4 chapters dismantled (Lawrence, Lynn, Haverhill, Boston)
  • 7 undocumented individuals apprehended and deported or in proceedings
  • 465 pages of supporting affidavit
  • 5 guilty pleas to date
  • 2 sentences imposed (10 years and 14 years)

The investigation involved more than a dozen federal, state, and local agencies — HSI, FBI, DEA, ATF, U.S. Marshals, IRS Criminal Investigation, Massachusetts State Police, and police departments in Lawrence, Andover, Boston, Haverhill, and Tewksbury, along with cooperation from U.S. Attorney's offices in New Hampshire and Maine.

It is being prosecuted by Assistant U.S. Attorney Philip A. Mallard of the Organized Crime & Gang Unit in the District of Massachusetts — which means one AUSA has primary responsibility for what is functionally a sprawling multi-year war against an entire criminal organization across three states.

What Comes Next

Twenty-four of the 26 newly charged defendants were taken into custody on the morning of June 9. Several previously charged defendants in the same docket still have pending cases. The RICO conspiracy charge alone carries a potential sentence of up to life in prison.

U.S. Attorney Foley's parting message was characteristically direct:

"If they try to resurrect themselves, we will take them out again."

The Trinitarios in Massachusetts aren't the first gang to operate under the delusion that structure makes you untouchable. That a "Magna Carta" is a shield rather than an exhibit. That saying "I choose violence" on a phone the FBI is listening to is an assertion of power rather than a confession of intent.

The record suggests otherwise. 465 pages of it.
